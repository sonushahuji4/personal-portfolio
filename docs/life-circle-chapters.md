**teaser:** March 2020. The world stopped. My career started.

**story:**

My final semester was supposed to be the celebration. Farewells, placements, a cap-toss photo I'd send my parents.

Instead: lockdown.

One Tuesday in March I walked out of DBIT and didn't come back as a student. No graduation ceremony. No final bike ride with friends. No hug with the friends I'd seen every day for four years. Just Zoom and uncertainty.

Placement season happened on video calls. Interviews in pajama bottoms and formal shirts. I remember my Cimpress interview — sitting at my desk at home, praying the wifi would hold, trying to explain recursion to someone who couldn't see my hands.

The offer came through email. I stared at it for five minutes before telling anyone. Then I told my mom, and she cried without knowing what Cimpress was.

I joined Cimpress in August 2020. My "first day" was a video call. I'd never been to the office. I wouldn't see my teammates' faces in person for over a year.

What COVID taught me: the world will pause. Your career will not. Keep showing up, even on days when "showing up" means opening a laptop in your bedroom.

**teaser:** From fresher to Senior Engineer. National Pen. 30% of revenue. Star of the Sprint.

**story:**

Two and a half years at Cimpress India. They took a fresher and turned him into an engineer.

**National Pen** — an e-commerce platform selling customized pens and merchandise across 20+ countries. Millions of users I'd never meet, buying pens with their name engraved. There's something weirdly beautiful about that.

First production deploy: I was terrified. I triple-checked the PR. Ran the tests four times. When the deploy went green, I closed my laptop and stared at the wall for five minutes. Then I made chai.

The mentor who pushed me hardest: **Apurv Moroney**. Now at Google. He didn't give me answers — he made me find them. I hated it in month one. I thank him every month since. The recommendation he wrote me later ("a keen eye for detail and a ready-to-learn attitude") — I printed that.

**Jinal Shah** managed me for two years. Her complaint? "He didn't chat much." She wasn't wrong. I was scared. But she made sure the work spoke for itself.

Features I shipped: Templates, Accessories, Premium products — they ended up contributing 30–40% of platform revenue. I didn't know that at the time. I was just trying not to break anything.

**Star of the Sprint, February 2023.** My last month. I got it for the Pay Later integration — a real-time WebSocket payment flow that shipped on time, didn't break, and boosted checkout completion. Best goodbye gift they could've given me.

The jump from SDE to Senior SDE happened in July 2022. My boss called me with the news. I pretended to be calm. I wasn't.

## Chapter 8

**id:** 8  
**number:** "08"  
**title:** "The Leap"  
**era:** "Joining Aerem · February 2023"  
**mood:** "red-orange"  
**icon:** "DoorOpen"  
**accentColor:** "#ff5b35"

**teaser:** Left a global company to join a 3-person team. Wild.

**story:**

Cimpress was comfortable. The paycheck was reliable. The team was great. I could have coasted for five years.

In January 2023, I got a call about Aerem — a solar fintech startup I'd never heard of. Three people. One office. One idea.

I took the meeting out of curiosity, not intent. I walked out of the meeting knowing I was going to say yes.

The conversation with my parents was hard. *"You're leaving a stable company for a startup?"* My mom asked. *"What if it doesn't work?"*

I said: *"What if it does?"*

My father didn't say much. He just nodded. That's how he approves of things.

First day at Aerem: I got handed a laptop and told *"pick the stack. design the schema. build the product."* I went home that night and didn't sleep. Not from excitement — from fear. They had trusted me with everything. I didn't know if I deserved it.

I started typing the next morning anyway. Sometimes that's all engineering is: starting to type even when you don't know if you deserve to.

## Chapter 9

**id:** 9  
**number:** "09"  
**title:** "Zero to One"  
**era:** "Aerem from scratch · 2023–2024"  
**mood:** "solar-gold"  
**icon:** "Sun"  
**accentColor:** "#ffb300"

**teaser:** Picked the stack. Designed the schema. Shipped the MVP. Closed a Series A.

**story:**

First week at Aerem was an empty repo. Just a git init. Nothing else.

I chose React + Node.js + PostgreSQL + AWS. Partially because I knew them. Partially because I could defend every choice. A startup's tech stack is a wager, and I bet on boring technology because boring technology ships.

The first schema I drew — I still have it. Napkin diagram. Solar plants, inverters, readings, alerts, customers. Four boxes connected by arrows. From that napkin, we grew to **500+ plants monitored**, **200+ B2B customers**, **2M+ daily IoT data points**.

The all-nighter that went into the Series A pitch deck: November 2023, two engineers, one week to build the demo. I wrote the real-time dashboard from scratch. The investors saw live inverter data streaming in and **INR 160 Crore** landed.

I was Employee #2 when the round closed. Now there are 50 of us.

The best part of zero-to-one isn't the glory. It's the moment when a customer calls to say their solar plant is working because of something you built. That happened to me for the first time in 2024. I hung up and sat quietly for a minute.

Hiring my first engineer was weird. Being on the other side of the interview table. Asking questions I used to dread. I tried to be the interviewer I wish I'd had — curious, patient, not trying to catch you out.

The team grew from 1 to 5 engineers. I wrote the code review culture myself. Two juniors became senior contributors in four months. I was proud of that in a way I wasn't of my own code.

**story:**

I started Scaler two months after joining Aerem. People thought I was crazy. "You're a founding engineer at a funded startup. Why do you need Scaler?"

Because being good at your job is different from being rigorous. I was shipping, but I couldn't always explain *why* my design was good. I wanted the vocabulary. I wanted the fundamentals.

**16 modules over two years.** DSA Intermediate, DSA Advanced (four modules), Databases & SQL, Backend LLD (three modules), HLD, Backend Capstone, Data Engineering, DSA for Competitive Programming, Product Management.

Hardest module: **High Level Design**. I thought I knew system design from work. I didn't. I was solving problems, not understanding patterns. Scaler made me understand patterns.

One DSA problem that broke me: [a specific LeetCode problem — Dynamic Programming? Tries?]. I spent three weekends on it. When I got it, I laughed at my kitchen table like an idiot.

**Mock interview, Round 2, Backend LLD.** I'd prepared. I thought I was ready. The interviewer tore my design apart in 15 minutes. I left the call shaken. I rebuilt the design that night. I cleared the next mock.

**624 LeetCode problems** as of today. Knight level. Top 1.85% globally. I didn't do it for the numbers. I did it for the habit. A problem a day, most days, for years. The compound interest of small things.

**story:**

It's 2026. I'm 29. Senior Engineer. Founding team at a Series A startup. Six years into a career that started with a Zoom interview in my bedroom.

What a typical day looks like: wake at 6:30, coffee, code review before the team's online, standup at 10, heads-down until 2, lunch (usually something my mom packed or I didn't cook), meetings, deep work, occasional incident, dinner, guitar for 20 minutes, sleep.

What I'm still learning: **staying curious**. Not curious for credentials — curious for joy. I want to be the senior engineer who's excited about new things, not threatened by them.

What I'd tell my 2016 self (first day at DBIT): *"You're going to be okay. You're going to be more than okay. Don't let the imposter syndrome win — it's lying to you. The thing you think you're bad at is the thing you're going to be known for in ten years."*

Engineers I look up to: Dan Abramov (kindness + rigor), Kent Beck (thinking clearly), Martin Kleppmann (depth). And the ones closer to me — Apurv, Siby, Ankur — who showed me that good engineers are good people first.

Outside code: I play guitar (badly), piano (worse), football (enthusiastically), hockey (historically), basketball (occasionally). I read. I'm trying to write. I'm trying to be a little less afraid of being bad at things.

What I want to build next: something where the code is in service of people, not the other way around. I'll know it when I see it.
